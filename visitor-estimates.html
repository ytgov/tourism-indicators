Highlights
  • From January to September 2024, the number of visitors to the Yukon is roughly on par with 2023. There was a 9.2% decrease in automobile visitors, but higher bus, plane, and train visitors.
  • In 2023, Yukon had an estimated 450,500 visits, down 8.3% from the all-time high of 491,300 in 2018. Visitation has rebounded significantly from the pandemic, reflecting renewed interest in travel to the territory.
  • It is expected to continue its upward trend as international travel recovers fully, and Yukon strengthens its position as a sought-after destination for its unique wilderness experiences, vibrant cultural offerings, and welcoming communities.
  • The visitation figures below show the results of past Visitor Exit Surveys, as well as an estimate based on border crossings and air arrivals.

Methodology

Data Inputs

To estimate the number of visitors to the Yukon, we use three main sources of data:

  • The Visitor Exit Survey 2022/23 (VES), Yukon Bureau of Statistics (VBS)
  • Statistics Canada International Border Crossings
  • Highways and Public Works Air Arrivals

The VES is our primary reference, providing detailed monthly visitor counts broken down by transportation type (bus, plane, road, or train). This survey serves as our baseline, or “truth,” for understanding visitor numbers.

Methodology

Using the VES as a starting point, we compare its monthly visitor data to border crossing figures and air arrival counts. This comparison allows us to calculate a ratio or percentage that represents how the border crossings and air arrival numbers relate to the survey data for each month. For example, if the VES reports 1,000 air visitors for May, and HPW reports 10,000 air arrivals, we calculate a ratio of 10%. This means that approximately 10% of air arrivals for that month are visitors.

These ratios can then be applied to border crossing and air arrival data for future months to estimate visitor numbers when survey data is not available. This method is defendable because it is based on reliable data sources and a clear calculation process. However, it is not fully scientific as it does not account for all factors influencing visitor trends. It is also based on 2022/2023 data, which means it should not be applied into the past and should be updated when the next visitor exit survey comes out.

Validation

To test this methodology, we compared the results of this method (490,496) with the visitor survey for 2017/2018 (491,300). This estimate was within 1%. However, when compared against the 2012 VES (317,200), this method significantly overestimated the number of visitors (388,250). Because of this, this method should not be applied into the past.

Limitations

While this method is straightforward, it has some limitations. One major gap is that the border crossing data does not capture all road traffic, particularly travelers entering the Yukon via the Alaska Highway, The Stuart Cassiar Highway, or the Dempster Highway.

Next Steps

This method is effective at estimating the number of visitors that have come to the Yukon given the data provided. It could also be used to predict annual visitor numbers based on a few months of input. Advanced statistical models, such as regression modeling or time-series analysis, could be implemented to come up with forecasted numbers.
